Seaborgium
Atomic Number: 106

Atomic Mass: ~271 (most stable isotope)

Electronegativity: ~1.3 (estimated)

Density: ~35 g/cm³ (predicted)

Melting Point: unknown (predicted ~2900 K)

Boiling Point: unknown

Electron Config: [Rn] 5f14 6d4 7s2

Discovery

Seaborgium was first synthesized in 1974 by scientists at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and independently confirmed at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory. It was named in honor of Glenn T. Seaborg, a key figure in the discovery of many transuranium elements.

Isotopes

Seaborgium has no stable isotopes. The most long‑lived is Sg‑271, with a half‑life of about 2.4 minutes.

Uses

- Purely scientific research
- Studies of superheavy element chemistry
- No practical applications due to short half‑life

Fun Fact

Seaborgium is one of the few elements named after a living scientist at the time of its naming.
