Cm
Curium
Atomic Number: 96

Atomic Mass: 247

Electronegativity: 1.3

Density: 13.51 g/cm³

Melting Point: 1613 K (1340 °C)

Boiling Point: 3383 K (3110 °C)

Electron Config: [Rn] 5f7 6d1 7s2

Discovery

Curium was discovered in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Ralph A. James, and Albert Ghiorso. It was named in honor of Marie and Pierre Curie for their pioneering work on radioactivity.

Isotopes

Curium has no stable isotopes. The most notable are Cm‑242 (half‑life 163 days) and Cm‑244 (half‑life 18 years).

Uses

- Power sources in space missions (radioisotope thermoelectric generators)
- Research in nuclear chemistry
- Neutron sources
- Limited applications due to radioactivity

Fun Fact

Curium was the first element named after a married couple — Marie and Pierre Curie.
